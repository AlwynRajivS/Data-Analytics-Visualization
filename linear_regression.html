Simple Linear Regression

Find the best-fit straight line through two variables and use it to predict future values. The foundation of all predictive modelling.

💡 What is Simple Linear Regression?

SLR models the linear relationship between one input variable (X) and one output variable (Y). The goal is to find a straight line that best describes how Y changes as X changes.

Core Equations Simple Linear Regression Formulas
1
Regression Equation
$$\hat{Y} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot X$$
The predicted value of Y for a given X. The line that minimises prediction errors.
2
Slope (β₁)
$$\beta_1 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})^2}$$
Rate of change of Y per unit increase in X. Numerator = covariance × n; denominator = variance of X × n.
3
Intercept (β₀)
$$\beta_0 = \bar{y} - \beta_1 \cdot \bar{x}$$
The predicted Y when X = 0. The regression line always passes through the point $(\bar{x},\, \bar{y})$.
4
R² — Coefficient of Determination
$$R^2 = 1 - \frac{SS_{res}}{SS_{tot}} = 1 - \frac{\sum(y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2}{\sum(y_i - \bar{y})^2}$$
Proportion of variance in Y explained by X. Ranges from 0 (no fit) to 1 (perfect fit).
5
RMSE & MAE
$$RMSE = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2} \qquad MAE = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}|y_i - \hat{y}_i|$$
Error metrics in the same units as Y. Lower = more accurate predictions.
⚠️ SLR assumes a linear relationship between X and Y. If the true relationship is curved, a different model is needed. Always plot your data first!
📚 Key Concepts Explained
β₀

Intercept

Where the regression line crosses the Y-axis. The predicted Y when $X=0$. A mathematical anchor — may not have practical meaning.

β₁

Slope

How much Y changes for every 1-unit increase in X. Example: salary increases by ₹5,200 per year of experience.

R²

Goodness of Fit

$R^2 = 0.87$ means 87% of Y's variation is explained by X. Closer to 1 = better fit.

OLS

Least Squares

Finds $\beta_0, \beta_1$ that minimise $\sum(y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2$. Mathematically optimal line through the data.

ε

Residuals

Residual $= y_i - \hat{y}_i$. A good model has small, randomly scattered residuals around zero.

RMSE

Error Metric

Average prediction error in Y's units. $RMSE = \sqrt{\frac{SS_{res}}{n}}$. Lower = more accurate.

🔢 Step-by-Step Calculation
1

Gather Data Pairs

Collect $(X_i, Y_i)$ pairs. Example: 30 employees each with experience $X$ and salary $Y$.

2

Compute Means

$\bar{x} = \frac{1}{n}\sum x_i$ and $\bar{y} = \frac{1}{n}\sum y_i$

3

Compute Slope β₁

$\beta_1 = \frac{\sum(x_i-\bar{x})(y_i-\bar{y})}{\sum(x_i-\bar{x})^2}$

4

Compute Intercept β₀

$\beta_0 = \bar{y} - \beta_1 \cdot \bar{x}$. The line passes through $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$.

5

Generate Predictions

$\hat{Y}_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i$ for each observation.

6

Evaluate Model

Calculate $R^2$, RMSE, and MAE. If $R^2 < 0.5$, the model has weak explanatory power.

Multiple Linear Regression

Model the relationship between two or more input variables and one output. Find what drives your outcome and predict with greater accuracy.

💡 What is Multiple Linear Regression?

MLR extends simple regression to include two or more independent variables. Each coefficient tells you the effect of that variable while holding all others constant — the partial effect.

X₁, X₂…Xₙ
Multiple Features
→
OLS Matrix
(XᵀX)⁻¹XᵀY
→
Predicted Ŷ
One Output
Core Equations Multiple Linear Regression Formulas
1
General Equation
$$\hat{Y} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_1 + \beta_2 X_2 + \cdots + \beta_p X_p$$
Each $\beta_k$ is the partial effect of $X_k$ on $Y$, holding all other variables constant.
2
Matrix Form (OLS Solution)
$$\boldsymbol{\hat{\beta}} = (\mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{X})^{-1} \mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{y}$$
Where $\mathbf{X}$ is the design matrix $(n \times (p+1))$ with a leading column of 1s. This gives the unique least-squares solution.
3
Design Matrix Structure
$$\mathbf{X} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & x_{11} & x_{12} & \cdots & x_{1p} \\ 1 & x_{21} & x_{22} & \cdots & x_{2p} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 1 & x_{n1} & x_{n2} & \cdots & x_{np} \end{bmatrix}$$
The first column of 1s corresponds to the intercept $\beta_0$. Dimensions: $n \times (p+1)$.
4
Adjusted R² (penalises extra variables)
$$\bar{R}^2 = 1 - \frac{(1-R^2)(n-1)}{n-p-1}$$
Unlike $R^2$, Adjusted $R^2$ only increases if a new predictor genuinely improves the model. $n$ = samples, $p$ = predictors.
5
Normal Equations (Equivalent)
$$(\mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{X})\,\boldsymbol{\hat{\beta}} = \mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{y}$$
Setting the gradient of the loss to zero yields these normal equations. Solving them gives the OLS solution via matrix inversion.

Simple Regression

1 predictor: $\hat{Y} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X$
Fits a 2D line
Easy to visualise on a chart
May miss important factors

Multiple Regression

2+ predictors: $\hat{Y} = \beta_0 + \sum \beta_k X_k$
Fits a hyperplane in $p+1$ dimensions
Requires Actual vs Predicted plot
More realistic, accurate model
📚 Key Concepts in MLR
β

Partial Coefficients

Each $\beta_k$ represents the change in $Y$ for a 1-unit increase in $X_k$, holding all other variables constant.

Xᵀ

Design Matrix

Matrix where each row is one observation. First column = 1s for $\beta_0$. Dimension: $n \times (p+1)$.

⁻¹

Matrix Inversion

The OLS solution requires inverting $(X^\top X)$. We use Gaussian Elimination — numerically stable.

R̄²

Adjusted R²

Penalises adding useless predictors: $\bar{R}^2 = 1 - \frac{(1-R^2)(n-1)}{n-p-1}$

VIF

Multicollinearity

When two predictors are highly correlated, it distorts coefficients. If $(X^\top X)$ is singular, inversion fails.

ε

Residual Analysis

Residuals $\varepsilon_i = y_i - \hat{y}_i$ should be randomly scattered around zero. Patterns indicate missing structure.

🔢 Step-by-Step: Matrix OLS Calculation
1

Build Design Matrix $\mathbf{X}$

Add a column of 1s as the first column. For $p=3$ features: $\mathbf{X}$ is $n \times 4$.

2

Transpose: $\mathbf{X}^\top$

Flip rows and columns. If $\mathbf{X}$ is $n \times 4$, then $\mathbf{X}^\top$ is $4 \times n$.

3

Compute $\mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{X}$

$(4 \times n) \cdot (n \times 4) \to (4 \times 4)$. Captures all correlations between features.

4

Invert $(\mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{X})^{-1}$

Gaussian elimination on the $4 \times 4$ matrix. Most computationally intensive step.

5

Solve $\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}$

$\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = (\mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{X})^{-1} \mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{y}$ gives $[\beta_0, \beta_1, \ldots, \beta_p]$.

6

Predict & Evaluate

$\hat{\mathbf{y}} = \mathbf{X}\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}$. Compute $R^2$, Adj. $R^2$, RMSE, MAE.
